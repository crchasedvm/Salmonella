Major Threat

Now an endemic disease causing significant outbreaks of scouring, abortion, and mortality in NZ dairy cattle.

Dairy cattle are a primary reservoir for zoonotic strains, directly linking to human cases in New Zealand.

The Threat: Pathogen & Transmission

This section explains how Salmonella causes disease and spreads on-farm. The primary route is fecal-oral, but the cycle is complex, involving carrier animals, environmental contamination, and various stressors.

Pathogenesis: From Ingestion to Systemic Disease

The disease begins when an animal ingests the bacteria. After surviving the stomach's acid, Salmonella uses specialized appendages to invade the cells lining the intestines. This invasion triggers a massive inflammatory response, causing severe damage to the gut wall. This damage leads to two key problems: the gut can no longer absorb water, and it begins to leak large amounts of fluid and protein. This results in the profuse, watery diarrhoea characteristic of the disease.

In vulnerable animals (like calves or stressed cows), the bacteria can break through the damaged gut wall and enter the bloodstream, a condition called septicemia. Once in the blood, the bacteria can travel to vital organs like the lungs, liver, and a pregnant cow's placenta, causing pneumonia, hepatitis, and abortion. The release of bacterial toxins (endotoxins) into the blood can lead to systemic shock, collapse, and rapid death.

The Cycle of Infection

The asymptomatic carrier animal is central to the spread of Salmonella. Click on the different stages below to understand how the infection is maintained and amplified within a herd.

1. Carrier Animal

Healthy-looking but infected animal acts as a reservoir.

2. Stress Trigger

Calving, transport, or diet change triggers shedding.

3. Environmental Contamination

Bacteria shed in faeces contaminate pasture, water, and feed.

4. Ingestion by Herdmates

Healthy animals ingest bacteria from the contaminated environment.

5. Clinical Disease

Sick animals shed massive amounts of bacteria, amplifying the outbreak.

6. New Carriers

Recovered animals can become new asymptomatic carriers.

On-Farm Risk Factors

Modern dairy intensification has created conditions that increase Salmonellosis risk. Click on the cards below to explore the key risk factors identified by New Zealand research.

Herd Size & Stocking Rate

Larger herds and higher stocking rates increase the concentration of potential carriers and pathogen load. A 100-cow increase can raise outbreak risk by 20%.

Mixing Stock

Purchasing/leasing stock or using off-farm grazing are major risks for introducing new strains or carrier animals into a naive herd.

Effluent Management

Applying effluent at rates over 15mm can increase outbreak risk six-fold by overloading pastures with pathogens.

Wildlife Presence

The presence of wildlife like deer can increase outbreak risk five-fold. Rodents and birds are also key vectors, contaminating feed and water.

Magnesium Supplementation

Coarse, pelleted, or prilled forms of magnesium oxide can raise rumen pH, allowing Salmonella to bypass the stomach's acid barrier and colonize the intestine.

Staff & Hygiene

In calf-rearing, each additional staff member can double the risk, highlighting the role of human movement as a vector between vulnerable groups.

Prevention Hub

Effective prevention is a multi-layered system. A single intervention is not enough. Use the tabs below to explore the core components of building herd resilience: Biosecurity, Vaccination, and Nutrition.

Foundational Biosecurity & Management

  • Closed Herd Priority: The best prevention. If not possible, source stock from high-health status farms only.
  • Strict Quarantine: Isolate all new arrivals for a minimum of 4 weeks. Observe for any signs of disease.
  • Pest & Wildlife Control: Implement a continuous program to control rodents and birds. Secure feed stores and maintain boundary fences.
  • Rigorous Hygiene: Handle sick animals LAST. Use an all-in/all-out system for calf pens with thorough disinfection. Never feed waste milk to calves.
  • Effluent & Grazing: Store effluent before application. Do not exceed 15mm application rates. Observe grazing stand-down periods, especially for transition cows.

Vaccination is the most effective tool to enhance specific herd immunity. In NZ, Salvexin¬Æ+B is the licensed quadrivalent vaccine, targeting S. Typhimurium, S. Bovismorbificans, S. Brandenburg, and S. Hindmarsh.

Primary Course (Unvaccinated Animals)

Two doses are required: a sensitiser followed by a booster at least 4 weeks later.

Annual Booster (Crucial Timing)

Give a single booster during the dry period (e.g., May). This ensures peak immunity during the high-risk calving period and boosts colostral antibodies for calf protection.

Calf Protection

Passive immunity from colostrum of vaccinated dams is the calf's first line of defense. For calves from unvaccinated dams, a primary course can begin from 4 weeks of age.

Nutritional Strategies to Mitigate Risk

Nutrition is a key tool for disease mitigation. This involves removing known triggers and using functional additives to enhance gut health and immunity.

  • Manage Magnesium: Avoid coarse/pelleted magnesium oxide. Use finely ground (200 mesh) powders or dose soluble forms into water to maintain a healthy gut pH.
  • MOS & Beta-Glucans: Derived from yeast cell walls. MOS binds to pathogens like Salmonella, preventing them from attaching to the gut wall. Beta-glucans prime the immune system to fight infection.
  • Bacillus Based Probiotics: Live beneficial bacteria that survive the stomach to colonize the gut. They out-compete pathogens for space and nutrients and can produce antimicrobial compounds.
  • Yeast Culture (Postbiotics): Fermentation products containing a mix of beneficial compounds. Strong evidence shows SCFPs can reduce diarrhoea, limit Salmonella colonization, and even reduce the pathogen's virulence and antibiotic resistance.

üö® Outbreak Response Plan

Despite best efforts, outbreaks can happen. A rapid, systematic response is critical to limit spread, save lives, and protect human health. Follow these steps immediately.

Phase 1: Immediate Actions (First 1-12 Hours)

  • Isolate Sick Animals: Immediately move any cow with fever or scours to a dedicated sick pen. This is the single most important step.
  • Contact Veterinarian: Essential for diagnosis, treatment protocols, and professional guidance.
  • Brief Staff & Enforce PPE: Inform all staff of the zoonotic risk. Mandate gloves and dedicated boots/overalls in the sick area.
  • Cease Feeding Waste Milk: Immediately stop feeding any milk from sick or treated cows to calves.

Phase 2: Diagnosis & Treatment (Day 1-3)

  • Prioritise Supportive Care: Aggressive fluid and electrolyte therapy (oral or IV) is the cornerstone of treatment and is often life-saving.
  • Use Antibiotics Judiciously: Antibiotics are for systemic cases (septicemia) and must be guided by a vet, ideally based on susceptibility testing to combat resistance.
  • Consider Outbreak Vaccination: Discuss with your vet about vaccinating healthy animals to reduce new cases.
  • Remove Sources: Safely dispose of aborted foetuses/placentas by deep burial. Inspect feed/water for contamination.

Phase 3: Decontamination & Monitoring

  • Maintain Strict Biosecurity: Continue all isolation and hygiene protocols until the vet declares the outbreak over.
  • Clean & Disinfect: Once sick pens are empty, thoroughly remove all organic matter, wash, and apply a broad-spectrum disinfectant.
  • Monitor At-Risk Groups: Closely watch transition cows and calves for any new signs of illness.

Protecting Human Health

Salmonellosis is a serious zoonotic disease. During an outbreak, the risk to farm staff and their families is extremely high. Protecting people is a top priority.

High-Risk Individuals

Young children, the elderly, pregnant women, and immunocompromised individuals are at much higher risk of severe disease. They must be prevented from having any contact with sick animals or the contaminated farm environment.

Non-Negotiable Safety Protocols

üß§ Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

Mandatory use of gloves, waterproof overalls, and dedicated gumboots when handling sick animals. Gear from the sick pen stays in the sick pen.

üßº Rigorous Hand Hygiene
